OCTOBER CRISIS

What happens when a sepratist group turns to violent means to achieve their goals?

About

The Front de Liberation du Quebec was formed in 1963 with the goal of complete independence of Quebec. The FLQ resorted to violence to garner their demands, using tactics such as robberies, bombings, and kidnappings. On October 5, 1970, the FLQ captured James Cross, a British trade commissioner. For his release, they demanded the release of FLQ prisoners, the publication of the FLQ manifesto, and the ability of FLQ members to leave for Cuba. Following this on October 10, Pierre Laporte, a Quebec minister, was also kidnapped by the FLQ. Premier Robert Bourassa eventually asked for military assistance in Quebec, and announced some concessions. On October 16, Pierre Eliotte Trudeau was the prime minister of Canada from 1968-1979. Trudeau generated a great amount of popularity during his electoral campaign, leading to the period being coined "Trudeaumania". Pierre Trudeau invoked the War Measures Act, criminalizing membership in the FLQ, suspending civil liberties, and allowing unauthorized arrests. On October 17, Pierre Laporte’s body was found strangled in the trunk of an abandoned car. In early December, James Cross was finally released in healthy condition.

Why is this defining?
The October Crisis was the sole occurrence of the Wartime Measures Act being invoked during peacetime. This highlights the increasing tension in Quebec during the period, and the desperation of the government to restore order to the province. In addition, daily life for many Canadians was disrupted by the futile acts of terrorism, such as bombings and kidnappings. The crisis also shed light upon the hopes of pro-seperatist citizens, who felt that violence was the method to adopt to break free from Canada. Eventually, it ended up implying that diplomatic action was the only solution, leading to the Quebec referendums.

Historical Significance
The October Crisis marks a major moment of turmoil in Canadian history. The bombings, kidnappings, and violence that occured during the crisis shows that Canada is far from immune to terrorist organization, should the cause arise. Despite the measures taken by the government slowing down the independence movement, the event still established a disconnect between Quebec and the rest of Canada that continues today. For instance, the continuous battle can be seen through the Bloc Quebecois. Furthermore, the crisis has raised the question of how far the government can extend restrictions in order to resolve a crisis, and has shown the tip of the iceberg of restraining civil liberties.
