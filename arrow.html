CANCELLATION OF THE AVRO ARROW

What happens when the government cancels a billion-dollar breakthrough aircraft program?

About

The Avro Arrow was commissioned by the Canadian government in 1953 in order to counter the threat of Soviet bombers attacking North America. The British aircraft manufacturing company A.V. Roe took on the task of producing the Arrow. More than 20 000 people were employed for this endeavor. In 1957, John Diefenbaker John Diefenbaker was the prime minister of Canada from 1957-1963, coined popularly as "Dief the Chief". was elected as prime minister, with the priority of cutting spending. Thus, the Arrow presented a challenge due to the high program cost of 1.1 billion dollars. Additionally, on October 4, 1957, the Soviets revealed their Sputnik satellite. This made the Arrow technology seem obsolete, given that the threat of space-borne ballistic missiles was now a reality. Furthermore, countries such as the US were coming up with superior aircraft in terms of altitude in range. In consideration of these factors, the Avro Arrow program was canceled by Diefenbaker on February 20, 1959. All aircraft were dismantled and blueprints destroyed.

Why is this defining?
The Avro Arrow was a major contribution to Canada’s identity as a respectable nation. A major source of pride for Canadians, the Arrow featured record-breaking technology and established Canada as an aviation superpower. Thus, the cancellation of the Avro Arrow dealt a major blow to the status of Canada. Leverage was also given to other nations, such as the United States as Canada no longer had its capabilities as an aviation power. The dismissal of the Avro Arrow sheds light upon a shameful reversal in Canadian history.

Continuity and Change
Although all the physical Avro Arrows were dismantled, it continued to leave an impact. The cancellation of the program left nearly 15 000 skilled workers unemployed, a number of which emigrated to other countries to provide their valuable skill set. A good portion of engineers left Canada to work for NASA, employing an instrumental role in the moon landing mission. In addition, they continued to contribute to further endeavors like the shuttle mission. To supplement the air force, Canada was forced to settle for used Voodoo jets from the United States. The spirit of the Avro Arrow lives on, however; reportedly, an entire Arrow was smuggled out and landed in Britain, and a piece of the nose is kept in the Aviation and Space museum. The dream the Avro Arrow was meant to ignite has also somewhat surfaced, as Canada now boasts the fifth largest aerospace industry in the world.
